# Problem Statement Template

A problem statement describes a real human situation where something isn't working. It is written before any solution is considered. If your statement implies a feature, a product, or a technology, rewrite it.

---

## Principles

1. **Name a real person, not a category.** "Small business owners" is not a person. A specific individual with a specific context is.
2. **Describe a real situation, not an assumed one.** The problem should come from research or direct observation — not from what you think users experience.
3. **Separate the problem from the solution.** If the statement could double as a brief for a specific feature, it's too narrow.
4. **Include the cost of the problem.** A problem without consequence has no urgency. Show what's at stake if nothing changes.
5. **Show your evidence.** A problem statement without a source is an assumption. State what you observed, how you observed it, and how often — so anyone reading it can judge how much weight to give it.
6. **Make it testable.** You should be able to look at a finished design and ask: did this solve the problem as stated?

---

## Criteria

A strong problem statement answers all five of the following:

| Dimension            | Question to answer                                         | Warning signs                                                           |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Who**              | Who specifically experiences this problem?                 | A demographic, a persona type, or "users" — not a real person           |
| **What**             | What are they trying to do, and what's getting in the way? | The obstacle is vague ("it's confusing") or missing entirely            |
| **When / Where**     | In what specific situation does this problem occur?        | No context — the problem floats free of any real moment                 |
| **Why**              | What outcome do they actually want?                        | The outcome described is a product behaviour, not a human goal          |
| **How do you know?** | What did you observe, how, and how often?                  | "We think", "we assume", "stakeholder feedback" without direct research |

---

## Forming Your Evidence

Provenance answers three questions:

- **What method produced this insight** — interview, observation, usability session, analytics, etc.
- **Who participated and when** — enough detail that someone else could assess the sample
- **How often the pattern appeared** — a single observation is a data point; a pattern across multiple participants and methods is a finding; a finding interpreted in context is an insight

A problem statement should be grounded at the insight level, not the data point level.

Further reading:
- [Data vs. Findings vs. Insights — Nielsen Norman Group](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/data-findings-insights-differences/)
- [Just Enough Research — Erika Hall](https://abookapart.com/products/just-enough-research)
- [Field Guide to Human-Centered Design — IDEO.org](https://www.designkit.org/resources/1)

## Forming Your Problem

Any format works as long as it covers all five criteria above. The format is a starting point, not the goal. If your team has a format that produces clear, testable statements, use that.

Three well-established references if you need a starting point:

---

### Point of View (POV) — d.school

```
[Person] needs [need] because [insight].
```

> Amara, a first-generation university student, needs a way to navigate financial aid processes because the forms assume knowledge her family has never had reason to develop.

[d.school Design Thinking Bootleg](https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources/design-thinking-bootleg)

---

### Insight + Tension — Shape Up / Basecamp

```
[Person] is trying to [job], but [obstacle], which means [consequence].
```

> Project managers are trying to get a read on overall progress, but the only way to see it is to open every task individually, which means there's no way to get a quick status without doing significant manual work.

[Shape Up by Ryan Singer](https://basecamp.com/shapeup)

---

### Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) — Bob Moesta

```
When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome].
```

> When I have a long, boring commute in the morning, I want something to keep me occupied and fill me up slowly, so I can make the drive feel shorter and avoid being hungry before lunch.

Note: JTBD leaves the "who" implicit — the person is defined by the situation, not named directly. If your team finds this uncomfortable, prepend a subject without breaking the format.

[Demand-Side Sales by Bob Moesta](https://www.demandsidesales.com)

---

## Checklist Before Moving On

- [ ] The person named is specific enough that a teammate could interview them
- [ ] The situation is concrete — it happens at a particular moment, not "in general"
- [ ] There is no product, feature, or technology mentioned
- [ ] The consequence of the problem is stated
- [ ] The insight is backed by direct observation or research, with a source and frequency noted
- [ ] Two people reading this independently would agree on what a solution needs to achieve
